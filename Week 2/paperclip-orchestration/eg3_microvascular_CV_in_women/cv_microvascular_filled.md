### Paragraph answer
Across the assembled literature, female-predominant coronary microvascular dysfunction (CMD) is best understood as the convergence of three partially independent strands rather than a single mechanism. (1) **Hormonal**: estrogen physiologically maintains endothelial NO production, dampens oxidative stress, modulates purinergic ATP→adenosine signaling, and restrains androgen-driven inflammation; its withdrawal at menopause (or earlier in premature ovarian insufficiency) shifts the coronary microcirculation toward an endothelial-dysfunctional, pro-inflammatory phenotype. (2) **Endothelial / structural**: women with INOCA show a distinct functional profile — higher resting coronary flow, lower coronary flow reserve, more microvascular spasm, and similar index of microcirculatory resistance versus men, who present more epicardial spasm. Roughly half of women with symptoms of ischemia and no obstructive CAD have CMD on invasive testing (WISE / WISE-CVD), and the contribution of inflammatory mediators (IL-6, ET-1) and local epicardial adipose tissue suggests systemic and paracrine drivers rather than purely vessel-autonomous disease. (3) **Autonomic**: direct human evidence is sparse but supportive — skin sympathetic nerve activity rises during ambulatory ischemic episodes in both Takotsubo and stable IHD cohorts (Borle 2024), and recent CMD reviews enumerate autonomic dysfunction alongside endothelial and VSMC dysfunction as a core mechanism. The strongest claim the evidence supports is that the female-predominant CMD phenotype is downstream of estrogen-conditioned endothelial biology, with autonomic tone as a likely modulator, and inflammation/metabolic load as the most consistent amplifier — making the "surface streets before the freeway" image clinically apt.

### Synthesis bullets
- The mechanistic literature does not endorse a single "female microvascular pathway." Reviews converge on a six-axis CMD model (endothelial, VSMC, structural remodeling, autonomic, inflammation, oxidative stress) and place sex differences primarily in the endothelial and autonomic axes.
- Estrogen-axis evidence in humans is mostly indirect: cohort observations that CMD prevalence and severity rise across the menopausal transition, plus mechanistic reviews on ER signaling and NO/purinergic pathways. Direct estrogen-replacement-and-microvascular-endpoint data is thin (this is the SQ3 gap, not an SQ1 gap).
- Autonomic-axis evidence in women specifically is the thinnest of the three strands. Borle 2024 is a small (n=32) Cedars-Sinai/WISE-lineage pilot. Reviews cite autonomic dysfunction by name but rarely tie it to women-specific data. Animal models of estrogen-autonomic-microvascular coupling are well-developed (ovariectomy + sympathetic stress); the human analog work is largely inferential.
- Empirically, the female CMD phenotype on coronary function testing differs from the male one: women — impaired CFR and microvascular spasm dominate; men — epicardial spasm dominates (Jansen 2021). This is one of the few primary-data findings that ties sex specificity to a measurable functional signature.
- A common framing across the reviews is that microvascular dysfunction in women is *systemic*: retinal microvascular density is lower in women with CMD (cross-domain biomarker), and adipose, inflammatory, and renal milieu correlate with coronary microvascular endpoints. This favors a "small vessels are one organ" model over coronary-isolated disease.

### Confidence read
- **Evidence quality:** Moderate. Multiple recent narrative reviews and a handful of human cohort studies anchor the female-CMD framing. The strongest empirical pieces are WISE/WISE-CVD substudies and the Jansen 2021 CFT comparison; the autonomic strand rests on smaller pilot work.
- **Direction of evidence:** Concordant on endothelial dysfunction + estrogen withdrawal as central; more variable on the relative weight of autonomic versus inflammatory drivers.
- **Key uncertainty:** Whether the autonomic axis is causal, parallel, or downstream of endothelial dysfunction in women. No human study in this corpus isolates sympathetic withdrawal or augmentation against a microvascular endpoint in a controlled design.

### Paragraph answer
The diagnostic literature divides into three tiers. **Tier 1 — invasive reference standard:** coronary function testing (Doppler-wire CFR, bolus thermodilution IMR, intracoronary acetylcholine provocation) is the canonical diagnostic for CMD and the basis for nearly every prevalence estimate in female-INOCA cohorts. **Tier 2 — non-invasive imaging validated against the reference:** transthoracic Doppler echocardiography CFVR (TTDE-CFVR) is the best-validated and most accessible — it correlates with invasive CFR, predicts MACE in both sexes, and is prognostically equivalent in women; cardiac PET-derived CFR is the non-invasive imaging "gold standard" but is cost- and access-limited; stress CMR with myocardial perfusion reserve index detects asymptomatic CMD in autoimmune-risk-enriched cohorts and is the leading emerging probe along with oxygenation-sensitive CMR (no contrast, no stress agent). **Tier 3 — accessible surrogates with less direct CMD validation:** retinal optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA) has pilot direct validation in women — retinal vessel density and inner perfusion density are lower in women with invasively confirmed CMD than without — plus larger asymptomatic-cohort correlations with subclinical coronary atherosclerosis. Heart rate variability and resting heart rate are autonomic-tone surrogates that predict coronary events in postmenopausal women at scale (WHI, n=129,135 for resting HR) but are not specific to microvascular endpoints. Coronary artery calcium is informative for macrovascular risk reclassification but is **not** a microvascular measure and a CAC=0 result does not exclude CMD — a critical distinction this digest must carry forward.

### Synthesis bullets
- The asymptomatic-screening validation gap is real: most direct CMD-detection validation is in symptomatic INOCA cohorts, not in asymptomatic women. Extrapolating to screening means assuming that a low CFVR/MPRI/PET-CFR in an asymptomatic woman carries the same prognostic weight it does in a symptomatic woman with no obstructive CAD — plausible but not directly demonstrated.
- Retinal OCTA is the most promising new screening adjunct because (a) it directly measures a microvascular bed, (b) it has direct correlation with invasive CMD in women (Shiromani 2025, small N), and (c) it is widely deployed in ophthalmology with no radiation or contrast. Its weakness is that the available studies are pilot-scale and the CMD-positive vs. CMD-negative effect sizes are modest.
- The "eye-as-window-to-heart" literature supports a systemic microvascular interpretation: retinal microvascular impairment in a woman is correlated with both subclinical coronary atherosclerosis and with invasively confirmed coronary microvascular dysfunction — consistent with the SQ1 systemic-microvascular framing.
- Autonomic surrogates (resting HR, HRV) are powerful in large female cohorts but are non-specific. They are best used as integration markers in a multi-modal screening pathway, not as standalone CMD detectors.
- CAC is the dominant clinical screening anchor in current practice for women in midlife (especially with early menopause as a risk enhancer), but its silence on microvascular disease must be named. A 50-year-old woman with CAC=0 and symptoms — or even without symptoms — can still have CMD.
- A practical screening pathway emerging from this evidence would be tiered: a high-risk woman (SQ4 features) gets a TTDE-CFVR or stress CMR; widely accessible retinal OCTA could function as an upstream microvascular flag; CAC plays its existing role for macrovascular stratification. PET and invasive CFT remain confirmatory.

### Paragraph answer
The menopausal transition is, in the available evidence, both a clinical hinge for cardiovascular risk and the moment when microvascular dysfunction becomes harder to hide. Across recent reviews and the SWAN cohort, the perimenopausal-to-early-postmenopausal window is associated with the SQ1-described mechanistic shift — estrogen withdrawal, endothelial impairment, sympathetic activation, and rising metabolic load. Vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes) are the most studied surface phenotype linked to subclinical disease: women with frequent hot flashes show reduced brachial flow-mediated dilation, greater carotid intima-media thickness, and more aortic calcification than women without — independent of measured estradiol level — and the SCAPIS cohort confirms this association at population scale in 2,995 peri/postmenopausal women. On hormone therapy, the consensus is durable: **MHT is not indicated for primary or secondary cardiovascular prevention.** WHI established that combined CEE+MPA increases CHD, stroke, breast cancer, and PE risk in older postmenopausal women; CEE-alone is neutral for CHD but increases stroke regardless of age. The timing hypothesis — that MHT initiated within ~10 years of menopause or before age 60 in symptom-burdened women is at least cardiovascularly neutral and may be modestly protective — is supported by Cochrane subgroup analyses and the Choi 2020 systematic review and is the basis for current symptom-driven HT use (NAMS 2022 position statement, ACOG-aligned). However, the entire HT-CV evidence base is **macrovascular**: the studies measure MI, stroke, CHD death, and CHD composites. Microvascular-specific endpoint data is limited to small mechanistic studies (Kallikazaros 2008 showed estrogen infusion improves coronary flow response and lowers ET-1 in postmenopausal women without CAD) and pathophysiology reviews (Sickinghe 2019 linking estrogen depletion to CMD and HFpEF). Translating macrovascular HT findings to "what does this mean for my patient's coronary microvasculature" requires careful extrapolation rather than direct evidence.

### Synthesis bullets
- The microvascular reading of menopause is best supported by SWAN's repeated finding that vasomotor symptoms track with vascular dysfunction biomarkers independent of estradiol — implying that the symptoms themselves may flag the underlying vascular phenotype better than serum hormone levels.
- The "perimenopause as clinical surfacing of pre-existing CMD" framing is supported but not proven. The evidence supports a temporal alignment (CMD prevalence rises across the transition; FMD declines; CAC increases). What is missing is a longitudinal study in which CMD is measured before and after the transition in the same women.
- Hormone therapy positioning has settled. The medical-society consensus across the recent reviews and the WHI/HERS legacy: HT is for symptom relief in early menopause (within ~10 years, age <60) in women without contraindications, not for cardiovascular prevention. Transdermal formulations are preferred for thromboembolic risk; oral estrogens elevate stroke risk regardless of timing.
- Microvascular-endpoint-specific HT evidence is the most important gap in this sub-question. The Kallikazaros study is small and >15 years old; SWAN/WHI did not pre-register CFR or MPRI as endpoints. A modern study using TTDE-CFVR or PET-CFR before and during early MHT in symptomatic women would directly close the gap, and would be different in kind from the macrovascular WHI/HERS literature.
- Risk-enhancing menopausal patterns to carry forward: (a) early menopause (<45) and premature ovarian insufficiency (<40) — recognized by AHA/ACC 2018 as ASCVD risk-enhancing factors; (b) frequent or persistent vasomotor symptoms — emerging as a possible CMD-flag phenotype; (c) menopausal weight redistribution / insulin resistance — amplifies endothelial dysfunction.

### Confidence read
- **Evidence quality:** High for macrovascular outcomes of MHT (large RCTs and meta-analyses). Moderate for vasomotor symptom-vascular association (multiple cohorts, consistent direction). Low for direct HT-microvascular endpoint linkage (one small mechanistic study; one mechanistic review).
- **Direction of evidence:** Convergent on the timing hypothesis and the macrovascular HT positioning. Convergent on perimenopause as a vulnerability window. Divergent on whether MHT, properly timed, has *coronary microvascular* benefit — this question is essentially unanswered.
- **Key uncertainty:** Whether MHT initiated in early symptomatic perimenopause improves coronary microvascular endpoints (CFR, MBF, MPRI). Adjacent uncertainty: whether the FMD-vasomotor-symptom association reflects microvascular biology more than macrovascular surrogate biology.

- Preeclampsia is the highest-yield reproductive-history marker for CMD risk in this corpus. The direct PET evidence (Honigberg 2024) shows postpartum CMD; the SOLVABLE finding in SLE women shows preeclampsia-amplified subclinical CVD. - Autoimmune rheumatic diseases provide the strongest direct microvascular evidence. The mechanism is endothelial dysfunction driven by chronic inflammation (already established as a CMD pathway in SQ1) — so screening these women with stress CMR or CFVR has both mechanistic and outcome support.
- PCOS evidence is heterogeneous because PCOS itself is heterogeneous (lean PCOS vs. metabolic-syndrome-PCOS). Outcome-level signals are confounded by the metabolic phenotype. ### Paragraph answer
The intervention literature for women with detected coronary microvascular dysfunction is unambiguously thin, and many of the best-designed trials are negative. The two largest randomized trials directly in this population — Bechsgaard 2018's ramipril RCT in normotensive women with CFVR-confirmed CMD (n=63) and CWIIST's ranolazine crossover trial in 128 mostly female CMD patients (Bairey Merz et al.) — both **failed** to demonstrate a primary effect on either microvascular endpoint (CFVR, MPRI) or symptoms. Ranolazine has a partially favorable meta-analytic story (Sheikh 2021, n=318 across six trials): CFR improved by 0.27 (95% CI 0.09–0.45) and several SAQ domains moved, but heterogeneity was high and the effect on angina frequency and treatment satisfaction was null. The PRIZE trial of zibotentan, a selective endothelin-A antagonist, was neutral for exercise duration and ischemia. WISE-CVD substudies of quinapril and PDE-5 inhibition are positive for CFR improvement but only in the subgroup with already-low baseline CFR — supporting precision/endotyping rather than blanket therapy. A 24-week comprehensive lifestyle intervention in overweight women with CMD (Bechsgaard 2020, n=62) improved angina symptoms by 9–21 SAQ points and produced 9.6 kg weight loss but did not move CFVR — a result that supports symptom-targeted lifestyle therapy while underlining that the microvascular dysfunction itself can be durable despite metabolic improvement. The most defensible synthesis is mechanism-stratified therapy: identify the dominant endotype (endothelial-dependent vs. non-endothelial-dependent, vasospasm vs. low-CFR), match the agent (e.g., calcium channel blockers and short-acting nitrates for vasospasm; ACE-inhibitor or PDE-5 inhibitor in patients with low baseline CFR; ranolazine for angina symptoms in low-CFR subgroup), and accept that the long-term outcome evidence is still pending — the WARRIOR trial (intensive medical therapy including aspirin, high-intensity statin, maximally tolerated ACEi in women with INOCA) is the field's bet on whether this strategy reduces MACE at 3 years.

### Synthesis bullets
- Blanket pharmacotherapy in unselected female CMD populations has not worked in any of the well-powered modern trials. Both ramipril and ranolazine missed their primary endpoints when given to mixed-endotype female CMD populations.
- The signal-from-the-failure is endotype-dependence. Quinapril, PDE-5 inhibition, and ranolazine all show signal in the subgroup with low baseline CFR, but not in the broader CMD population. This implies an under-recognized heterogeneity problem: "CMD" is a phenotype, not an endotype, and trials need to randomize *within* endotypes.
- Lifestyle intervention improves symptoms without improving CFVR. Bechsgaard 2020 is the cleanest demonstration: 9.6 kg weight loss, 9–21 SAQ-point gains, and no CFVR change. This suggests symptom-targeted lifestyle therapy is worthwhile *for symptoms*, but is not a mechanism-correcting intervention in the microvascular sense.
- Autonomic-targeted interventions (paced breathing, HRV-guided training, vagal tone interventions) — explicitly named in the scope — **did not appear in this corpus** as RCTs in CMD populations. This is a genuine evidence gap, not a search failure (recovery searches did not produce any RCT-quality results in the autonomic-targeted lane).
- Estrogen-based therapy is conspicuously absent as a CMD intervention despite the mechanism story in SQ1. The HT literature (SQ3) is macrovascular-endpoint; HT-and-CFR trials of any size do not exist in this corpus.
- The WARRIOR trial (per Marano/Wei/Bairey Merz 2023) is the awaited evidence on whether the "intensive medical therapy package" (aspirin, high-intensity statin, max-tolerated ACEi) improves MACE at 3 years in women with INOCA. Result pending; this is the most important upcoming evidence in the field.

### Confidence read
- **Evidence quality:** Low-to-moderate. A small number of well-conducted RCTs (mostly null on primary endpoints), one meta-analysis with high heterogeneity, several positive but small WISE substudies, and no autonomic-targeted intervention data.
- **Direction of evidence:** Convergent on endotype-stratified rather than unselected therapy. Convergent on lifestyle improving symptoms without correcting CFR.
- **Key uncertainty:** Whether *any* intervention durably corrects coronary microvascular dysfunction at the microvascular-endpoint level. WARRIOR will provide the first large-scale MACE signal, but is not designed to read out at the CFR/MPRI level.

## Cross-cutting observations

- **The "small vessels are one organ" framing emerges across SQ1, SQ2, and SQ4.** Retinal microvascular density correlates with coronary microvascular function (Shiromani 2025); renal microvascular dysfunction (albuminuria) correlates with coronary CMD in WISE-CVD (PMC4423851); pericardial-fat-driven inflammation correlates with coronary endothelial flow (Landes 2024); and chronic systemic inflammatory disease (autoimmune rheumatic) drives coronary microvascular dysfunction (PMC11371758, PMC10266277). Treating microvasculature as a coherent system rather than coronary-isolated is supported by multiple independent measurement modalities.
- **The microvascular-versus-macrovascular endpoint problem.** The HT literature (SQ3) is built almost entirely on macrovascular composite endpoints (MI, stroke, CHD death, all-cause mortality). The HT trials did not pre-register CFR, MBF, or MPRI. The risk-stratification literature (SQ4) is dominantly macrovascular except for autoimmune-rheumatic-disease CMR studies and the Honigberg preeclampsia PET. The intervention literature (SQ5) is mixed but uses CFVR/CFR/MPRI as primary endpoints in most CMD-specific trials. So the field's evidentiary backbone is partial: we have macrovascular HT data and microvascular intervention data, but not microvascular HT data — and almost no integrative endpoint matching.
- **Endotyping is doing the heavy lifting where intervention works.** Several positive findings in SQ5 are subgroup signals in low-baseline-CFR populations (WISE-CVD quinapril and PDE-5 substudies; CWIIST ranolazine in CFR<2.5 subgroup) rather than unselected CMD populations. This argues that "CMD" is a phenotype that aggregates multiple endotypes (endothelial-dependent vs. non-endothelial-dependent; vasospasm-dominant; impaired-CFR-dominant) and that interventions only work in their matched endotype.
- **Autonomic axis: identified mechanistically, absent therapeutically.** SQ1 documents a working hormonal-autonomic-microvascular model. SQ2 identifies HRV and resting heart rate as scalable autonomic-tone surrogates that predict CV events in postmenopausal women at population scale (WHI n=129,135). But SQ5 found no RCT-quality autonomic-targeted intervention (HRV biofeedback, paced breathing, vagal stimulation) in CMD women. This is a defined evidence gap — the mechanism story justifies a trial that does not yet exist.
- **PCOS evidence is the only sub-question with a clear within-question conflict.** PMC10876862 and PMC3693634 frame PCOS as a CV risk-enhancing condition. PMC7003818 (Wekker 2020) found cIMT *lower* in middle-aged PCOS women than controls with equivalent 10-year Framingham risk. Reconciliation: PCOS-specific CV risk is plausibly mediated by metabolic phenotype rather than an independent vascular signature, and addressing the metabolic axis is the most defensible response.
- **WARRIOR is the most consequential pending evidence.** The trial is designed to test whether intensive medical therapy (aspirin + high-intensity statin + maximally tolerated ACEi) reduces MACE at 3 years in women with INOCA — exactly the population where current data is null or thin. Its result will likely reshape SQ5 once published.
- **Notable absences:** no large screening RCT in asymptomatic women using any of the SQ2 modalities; no HT-and-microvascular-endpoint trial in this corpus despite the timing-hypothesis mechanistic literature; no autonomic-targeted intervention RCT in CMD; no published WARRIOR primary result in the timeframe of this corpus; very limited African/Asian women–specific evidence outside of one Korean diabetes cohort and one Mexican SLE cohort.

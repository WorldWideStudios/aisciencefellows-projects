## Clinical question (verbatim from intake)

Where does cardiovascular disease in women actually begin — anatomically, mechanistically, and temporally — and what does that imply for screening, surveillance, and intervention across the lifespan? Specifically, why is microvascular disease the predominant axis of female cardiovascular pathology, and what can be done about it?

**Context from team discussion:** This digest extends a prior round on sex differences in cardiovascular pathophysiology. Round 1 covered estrogen as cardiac modulator, atypical female ischemic presentation, perimenopause as a vulnerability window, cycle-phase variation, and pregnancy as a cardiovascular stress test. Round 2 deepens specifically on the microvascular axis: why female cardiovascular disease appears to begin in the microvasculature and progress to large-vessel involvement (the "surface streets before the freeway" model), how to detect it asymptomatically, how the perimenopausal transition and hormone therapy positioning fit into the picture, how risk-enriched populations (PCOS, autoimmune inflammatory pathology, adverse reproductive history) modify screening, and what interventions have evidence in this space. Structural and hypoestrogenic-state questions raised in the same meeting are being handled as separate one-off Paperclip passes outside this digest.

## Sub-Question 2: What modalities have been studied for detecting asymptomatic microvascular disease in women?

**Scope:** Validation literature for diagnostic modalities targeting asymptomatic coronary or systemic microvascular disease in women. Known candidates include retinal vascular imaging, heart rate variability, coronary flow reserve, and coronary CT angiography versus coronary artery calcium. The question is intentionally open — surface any modality with a validation literature in this population, including ones outside this list. Prioritize non-invasive modalities suitable for screening; invasive measurements (e.g., catheter-based coronary flow reserve) are relevant only as reference standards.
